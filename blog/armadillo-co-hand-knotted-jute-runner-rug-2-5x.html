Frequently Asked Questions

Does this jute runner work in high-traffic areas like main hallways?

It depends on your definition of 'high-traffic.' In moderate traffic (families walking through a few times daily), it holds up well for 5-7 years. I tested one in a primary entryway that gets 10+ passes daily, and after six months, you could see visible wear patterns in the pile. If your hallway gets that kind of intense foot traffic, consider upgrading to a thicker jute blend or accepting that you'll need to rotate it periodically.

How does the price compare to other natural fiber runners?

Armadillo & Co positions itself as a mid-to-premium option. You can buy basic jute runners from Amazon or Wayfair for $60-$100, but they're typically machine-made and feel thinner. Luxury hand-knotted options (from brands like Ruggable) run $300-$500. Armadillo & Co sits in the $150-$300 range, which is fair for hand-knotted jute if you value visible craftsmanship, but it's definitely not a budget play.

Is the shedding really as bad as some reviews mention?

Yes, but it's temporary and manageable. Expect light shedding for the first 2-3 weeks—you'll notice loose fibers when you vacuum or shake it out. By week 4-5, shedding drops dramatically. One honest tip: vacuum it outside or in a well-ventilated space during the break-in period, and don't place it adjacent to light-colored furniture you're protective about. The shedding stops, but rushing past it is unrealistic.

Will this rug work on hardwood floors without slipping?

Jute's natural texture provides decent grip on hardwood, though it's not perfect. If you have older, highly polished hardwood and plan to place this in a main walkway, I'd recommend adding a non-slip rug pad underneath (around $15-$20). On most standard hardwood or vinyl plank flooring, the friction is sufficient without additional tools.

What's the best way to clean this runner?

Vacuum with a beater-bar setting set to high (so the brush doesn't pull threads too aggressively) every 3-5 days. For spot cleaning, use a dry method first: blot spills immediately with a clean, dry cloth. If moisture is needed, use minimal water with a mild detergent solution and allow it to air dry completely. Avoid soaking it or using a wet/dry vacuum, which can compromise the jute fibers and create mildew risk.
