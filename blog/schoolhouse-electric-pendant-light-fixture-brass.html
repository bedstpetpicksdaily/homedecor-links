Frequently Asked Questions

How does Schoolhouse Electric compare to Article or West Elm pendant lights?

All three occupy the same design space and similar price range ($250–400). Schoolhouse Electric edges out purely on brass durability and minimalist design longevity. Article tends toward trendier finishes that feel dated faster. West Elm offers more size and color variety, which matters if you're matching existing decor. For pure investment value, Schoolhouse Electric wins; for flexibility, West Elm edges ahead.

Can I install this myself without an electrician?

Most homeowners can, yes—if your home has existing light fixture wiring and you're comfortable with basic electrical safety (turning off breakers, testing for live wires). The Schoolhouse Electric pendant typically requires mounting the canopy, connecting three wires, and securing the fixture. Budget 30–45 minutes. If you've never touched electrical work, hire someone ($150–200); it's worth the peace of mind. Don't guess on this one.

Will the brass tarnish or require constant polishing?

Yes, it will develop patina—that's the aesthetic appeal for most buyers. You don't need to polish it unless you prefer shiny brass. Wipe it down occasionally with a dry cloth to slow tarnishing if you prefer the original finish. Some people intentionally accelerate the patina with vinegar because the aged look is genuinely more sophisticated than raw brass. This isn't a maintenance headache; it's a feature, not a bug.

What's the real difference between this and budget pendants under $50?

Materials and permanence. Budget pendants use thin plated metal that corrodes, flakes, and looks tired in 2–3 years. You'll replace them. Schoolhouse Electric's solid brass develops character and lasts 10+ years. If you're renting or you redecorate constantly, skip it. If this is your house, your investment, your decade-long kitchen—the math favors spending now and forgetting about it.
